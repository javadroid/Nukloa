WHITEPAPER EXECUTIVE SUMMARY


ABSTRACT

The efficiency and economics of large-scale storage have fundamentally changed as a result of decentralized cloud storage. Users can store and exchange data without relying on a third-party storage service when central control is removed. Decentralization increases object storage's security, read performance, and privacy while reducing the chance of data failures and outages.
Additionally, decentralization enables market forces to optimize for cheaper storage at a faster rate than any single provider could provide. Any particular implementation must take care of a number of specialized duties, such as security, compatibility, resistance against malicious actors, favorable economics for both providers and end users, and incentive setting. We offer a modular architecture for thinking about these duties and for developing our distributed storage network based on our experience with petabyte size storage systems. We also provide details on the framework's initial concrete implementation.

COLLOQUY

The world's expanding data requirements may be addressed via decentralized cloud storage, which has gained popularity. The amount of digital data produced worldwide doubles every year, and by 2024, some predictions predict it will exceed 30 zettabytes annually. At the same time, the vast majority of storage devices are working at less than 15% of their potential, and over the past three years, the cost of cloud storage has decreased by less than 8% annually. Furthermore, the traditional cloud model suffers from serious security, availability, and performance problems, especially in areas far from important data centers.
Decentralized cloud storage's inherent advantages can meet these requirements. Decentralized cloud storage stands out as a fresh, efficient, and cost-effective option by making use of underutilized, existing hard drives and bandwidth while maintaining Nodes that are comparable to conventional data centers. A decentralized strategy also enables us to build a system that is substantially more resilient to harmful or unreliable actors and significantly more durable.
Several important market areas can be addressed by the Nukleon decentralized orbital, especially those pertaining to long-term archive storage and S3-compatible object storage. To handle a far wider range of use cases, from fundamental object storage to content delivery networks, we are developing a system (CDN). The Nucleon decentralized orbital is the next step in cloud storage and will play a significant role in shaping and propelling innovation in the emerging web 3.0 era.

NUKLEON ORBITAL IMPROVEMENT BASED ON EXPERIENCE

Simplicity has been given top priority throughout the design process by our team. The majority of the network uses tried-and-true, frequently-applied technologies but employs them in novel ways. Our dedication to functionality includes our choice not to store files or metadata on a blockchain or other distributed ledger. Platforms with horizontal scaling, like AWS S3, are common among storage users and improve performance when more hardware is added. Distributed ledgers, however, find it difficult to accomplish this, thus we avoid utilizing them for actual data storage.
The Nukleon orbital uses erasure codes for resilience rather than replication, which is another important point of differentiation. Replication is a subpar option as a method to ensure resilience because bandwidth is a limiting issue on decentralized cloud storage networks. We have discovered that systems that use erasure codes to achieve six nines of durability use five times less storage capacity than systems that use replication to achieve the same durability level. This finding is based on our research and experience running our previous network, which scaled to a size greater than any other decentralized cloud storage network in the world.

Many platforms fall short in properly addressing these architectural limitations, leading to the delivery of decentralized cloud storage networks with excessive latency, poor file durability, high costs, and subpar performance.

NUKLEON ORBITAL DESIGN

We have created a unique design with eight parts that offers the best decentralized storage implementation. The architecture we describe performs within the parameters of the design restrictions, offers the fundamental capabilities anticipated, and transfers savings to storage users. The framework of components discussed in this white paper are:

    Storage nodes
    Peer-to-peer communication and discovery
    Redundancy
    Metadata

This framework is crucial to the overall Nukleon platform, and we don't anticipate it or its components changing as the network develops and grows. Because of this, we do not believe that a major overhaul of the network would be necessary; rather, we anticipate that the actual implementation of the various components will change. As this happens, we'll update the white paper as necessary.
Three separate areas of the network have these eight components.

    An Uplink is any software or service which invokes LibUplink in order to communicate with Satellites and Storage Nodes. Examples of Uplinks include the Uplink CLI and the Gateway.
    The Storage Node, which stores the data for the network. Each one is independently operated and does not share bandwidth, power or other resources.
    The Satellite, which operates as a heavy client that connects Uplinks to the Storage Node network and manages metadata for files. It also handles file audits, repair and other crucial network tasks.
    There will be many satellites on the network and companies and community members will be able to operate their own satellites as well.

BRIDGING OPEN SOURCE AND DECENTRALIZATION

In addition to being open source, our new orbital gives open source businesses financial flexibility by allowing them to profit each time a user stores data in the cloud. This helps Nukleon increase platform adoption among the creative open source community while also assisting open source businesses interested in making money from the use of their products in the cloud.
Through connectors developed with the help of each open source partner, the network makes the new program possible. The connectors keep track of data utilization by user or storage bucket. A portion of the revenue made when data passes via one of these connections is donated to the appropriate project, and the open source company is given credit for the usage. Revenue is also received by partners for network capacity utilization.
The new Nukleon white paper will be updated regularly as new functionality is made possible by developments in research and technological advancements. We predict that the core eight components will remain the same, while their concrete implementation will evolve to maximize security, dependability, efficiency, performance and take use of other benefits.
